Expressions of Time

One of the most common illogical uses of prepositions is in expressions that denote when or for how long something occurs.

For expressing the duration of an action, the preposition to use is generally i.

  • Jeg har lært norsk i nesten tre år: I have been learning Norwegian for almost three years.
  • Jeg spilte fløyte i to år: I played flute for two years.

But, if we're saying how long we have not done something, then we need to use på.

  • Jeg har ikke sett henne på årevis: I have not seen her in years.
  • Jeg har ikke spist noe på ti timer: I haven't eaten anything in ten hours.

We also need to use på when talking about the total amount of time something takes to achieve a goal.

  • Jeg kan vinne spillet på to timer: I can beat the game in two hours.
  • Jeg lagde pizzaen på tretti minutter: I made the pizza in thirty minutes.

And, we use på again when talking about days of the week.

  • Vi møtes på mandag: We're meeting up on Monday.
  • På søndager drikker jeg øl: On Sundays, I drink beer.

But, we use i when talking about months.

  • Vi møtes i mars: We're meeting up in March.
  • I juli drikker jeg øl: In July, I drink beer.

Then we have situations where we talk about points in time relative to the current time using durations: om for the future and for ... siden for the past.

  • Vi møtes om to timer: We're meeting up in two hours (from now).
  • Vi møttes for to timer siden: We met up two hours ago.
