Grammatical Gender

Like many languages, Norwegian has the concept of grammatical gender. Each noun belongs to one of three grammatical genders: masculine, feminine or neuter.

It's important to remember that these really have nothing to do with biological gender. It might help knowing that the word gender comes from the same roots as genus and genre. That is to say that the word really just means a group or classification.

A simple example of this is the word for mom: (en) mamma. This is a masculine word in Bokmål.

It's generally recommended to learn all nouns with their indefinite articles. Don't just remember mamma (mom), but remember en mamma (a mom) instead.

  • Masculine (en): en ball, a ball
  • Feminine (ei): ei and, a duck
  • Neuter (et): et hus, a house

The gender of a word factors into many things, so it's important that you learn it.

Common Gender

To make things even more confusing, expressing all feminine words in common gender (masculine) forms is allowed in Bokmål. So, you can say both ei and and en and.

This isn't an all or nothing thing either. Certain dialects will prefer to express certain feminine nouns in common gender and others in feminine forms. Additionally, using the common gender indefinite article en is fairly common even if the word is expressed in feminine definite form. So, you may see en and but anda (the duck) rather than anden.

My personal recommendation is to learn and use feminine forms for all feminine words until you find yourself living in a particular area of Norway. Then you can adopt local use as you are exposed to it.
