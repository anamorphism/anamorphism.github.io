Demonstratives

Demonstratives are the words this, that, these and those in English.

Some languages have them, others don't. (Un)fortunately for us, Norwegian does. Why this might be considered unfortunate will hopefully become apparent later.

The demonstratives in Bokmål are dette, denne, det, den, disse and de.

We use these words in two ways in both languages: as determiners and as pronouns.

Determiners

A determiner is a word that helps us determine (see what I did there?) which object or objects we are specifically talking about out of all objects of the same type.

If we say "ball" we could be referring to any ball, but if we say "this ball" or "that ball" then it becomes clearer as to which ball we're talking about.

The demonstrative determiners pretty much work the same way grammatically in both English and Norwegian. They are the first words in a noun phrase, that is to say any attributive adjectives will come after the determiner and before the noun itself. They must also match count of the noun in both languages: this, that, dette, denne, det and den for singular; and these, those, disse and de for plural.

The only real difference is that you also need to worry about grammatical gender in Norwegian in the case of singular nouns: dette and det for neuter, and denne and den for masculine and feminine.

Another thing to mention is that noun phrases starting with demonstrative determiners are definite. We can think of it as though we're defining which specific thing or things we're talking about. In English, this isn't such a big deal since we don't decline words based on definiteness, but in Norwegian we do. So, just make sure to remember to put any attributive adjectives and the noun itself in definite form.

  • dette huset: this house
  • dette røde huset: this red house
  • det huset: that house
  • det røde huset: that red house
  • denne ballen: this ball
  • den ballen: that ball
  • denne anda: this duck
  • den anda: that duck
  • disse hus[ene|a]/ballene/endene: these houses/balls/ducks
  • de hus[ene|a]/ballene/endene: those houses/balls/ducks

"But, Damien, doesn't det røde huset mean the red house?"

It sure does. We can either rely on context to make things clear or add additional words like der to really drive the point home.

  • det røde huset der: that red house / the red house there
  • den lille jenta der: that little girl / the little girl there
  • de store ballene der: those big balls / the big balls there

Pronouns

This is where things get a bit unfortunate for those of us that are native English speakers or know English well.

So, Norwegian actually pays attention to whether nouns have been introduced into the context of the conversation or text. The bit that trips up us English speakers is that we need to remember to use the singular neuter form of the demonstrative pronoun in all situations where the noun hasn't been introduced yet in Norwegian.

  • Dette er de viktigste egenskapene for ledere nå: These are the most important traits for leaders now.
  • Det er egenskapene jeg liker: Those are the traits I like.

Even though the pronoun represents a masculine word (en egenskap) in this sentence, and even though we're referring to a plural use of that noun, we haven't introduced what we're talking about yet to the context at hand. Thus, we need to use dette or det respectively, the singular neuter form of the demonstrative. In English, we still match count and use these or those even though we don't know what we're talking about yet.

Once the noun is in context, however, we switch to matching gender and count.

It's a bit difficult to show examples of this, as we often switch from using a demonstrative pronoun to a more standard pronoun in subsequent sentences in both languages: it or they in English, and den, det or de in Bokmål. However, we may see something like the following.

  • Dette er alle knivene. Denne kniven er den billigste, men denne er skarpere: These are all the knives. This knife is the cheapest (one), but this (one) is the sharpest.

We know we're talking about knives by the time we get to the third demonstrative. It might help to think about it as just not wanting to repeat the word knife or kniven over and over again. We often insert a one in English in these situations.

Stepping back a bit ... it's also worth mentioning that the second example above could also be translated as "they are the traits I like." This same "singular neuter" rule applies when using it and they (den, det and de).
